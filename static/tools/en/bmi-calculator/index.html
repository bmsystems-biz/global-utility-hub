❓ Frequently Asked Questions

BMI (Body Mass Index) is a number calculated by dividing weight in kilograms by height in meters squared. Recommended by the WHO, it's the most widely used screening tool to assess whether an adult's weight is healthy.
According to WHO, 18.5–24.9 is normal. The Asian standard (used in Korea, Japan, and China) is stricter: 18.5–22.9 is normal, 23–24.9 is overweight, and 25+ is obese.
BMI can be misleading for athletes (high muscle mass makes BMI appear high without excess fat), pregnant women, adults over 65, and growing children. Direct body fat measurement is more reliable in these cases.
Research shows Asian populations have higher body fat percentages and greater risk of metabolic diseases (diabetes, hypertension) at the same BMI compared to Western populations. Countries like South Korea, Japan, and China therefore apply lower thresholds.
A sustainable approach combines moderate calorie reduction with regular aerobic exercise. Aim for 0.5–1 kg of gradual weight loss per week rather than rapid changes. Always consult a doctor or dietitian before starting a weight-loss plan.
