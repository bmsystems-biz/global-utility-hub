## Why Crash Diets Don't Work Long-Term

Extreme calorie restriction causes rapid weight loss initially — but much of that loss is muscle and water, not fat. The side effects:

- **Muscle loss**: Reduced muscle mass lowers your resting metabolic rate
- **Metabolic adaptation**: Your body burns fewer calories to compensate
- **Rebound weight gain**: Most dieters regain the weight within 2 years

The evidence consistently shows that moderate, sustained changes outperform aggressive short-term interventions.

## The Calorie Math

One kilogram of body fat contains approximately 7,700 kcal. To lose 0.5 kg/week (the recommended healthy pace), you need a daily deficit of about 550 calories — achievable through a combination of eating less and moving more.

**Safe target**: 0.5–1.0 kg per week

## Dietary Principles That Work

### Prioritize Protein
Protein increases satiety, preserves muscle during weight loss, and has a higher thermic effect than carbs or fat. Target 1.2–1.6g of protein per kg of body weight per day. Good sources: chicken breast, fish, eggs, tofu, legumes.

### Reduce Refined Carbohydrates
White rice, white bread, and sugary drinks cause blood sugar spikes followed by crashes that trigger hunger. Replace with whole grains, vegetables, and legumes for more stable energy.

### Don't Fear Fat — Choose Wisely
Healthy fats (avocado, olive oil, nuts) promote satiety and support hormonal health. Saturated and trans fats should be limited.

### Volume Eating
Low-calorie, high-volume foods (leafy greens, cucumber, broth-based soups) let you eat satisfying portions without excess calories.

## Exercise: What Actually Moves the Needle

### Cardio for Calorie Burn
Aim for 150 minutes/week of moderate cardio (brisk walking, cycling, swimming). At 300 calories per 45-minute session, this adds up to roughly 0.25 kg of additional weekly fat loss.

### Strength Training for Metabolism
Resistance training 2–3 times per week preserves and builds muscle. Since muscle burns more calories at rest than fat, this raises your baseline calorie burn permanently.

### NEAT (Non-Exercise Activity Thermogenesis)
The calories burned from non-gym movement — walking, standing, fidgeting — can be substantial. Simple changes:
- Take the stairs
- Walk during phone calls
- Stand while working
- Park farther away

## Monitoring Progress Without Obsessing

- Weigh yourself weekly, same time (morning, after bathroom, before eating)
- Track trends over 4-week periods, not day-to-day fluctuations
- Also monitor non-scale metrics: energy levels, sleep quality, clothing fit, blood pressure

## When to Seek Professional Help

If your BMI is 30+ or you have related conditions (Type 2 diabetes, sleep apnea, hypertension), work with a doctor, registered dietitian, and potentially a licensed exercise physiologist for a medically supervised plan. Don't self-prescribe significant dietary or exercise interventions without professional guidance.
