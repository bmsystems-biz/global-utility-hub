## What Is BMI?

Body Mass Index (BMI) is a number calculated from your height and weight. Developed in the 19th century by Belgian mathematician Adolphe Quetelet, it became the WHO's recommended screening tool for assessing adult weight status.

## The BMI Formula

```
BMI = Weight (kg) ÷ Height (m)²
```

**Example**: Height 170cm (1.70m), Weight 65kg
```
BMI = 65 ÷ (1.70)² = 65 ÷ 2.89 = 22.5
```

## BMI Classification: WHO vs. Asian Standard

| Category | WHO Standard | Asian Standard |
|----------|--------------|----------------|
| Underweight | Below 18.5 | Below 18.5 |
| Normal | 18.5 – 24.9 | 18.5 – 22.9 |
| Overweight | 25.0 – 29.9 | 23.0 – 24.9 |
| Obese Class I | 30.0 – 34.9 | 25.0 – 29.9 |
| Obese Class II | 35.0+ | 30.0+ |

## Why Does the Asian Standard Differ?

Research consistently shows that Asian populations carry a higher proportion of body fat at equivalent BMI values compared to Western populations. Specifically, at the same BMI, Asian adults show:
- Higher visceral (abdominal) fat
- Greater risk of Type 2 diabetes and cardiovascular disease
- Earlier onset of metabolic syndrome

This is why countries including South Korea, Japan, China, and Singapore use stricter BMI thresholds.

## When BMI Is Not Accurate

BMI has well-known limitations:

- **Athletes**: High muscle mass raises BMI without excess fat
- **Pregnant women**: Normal weight gain affects BMI without reflecting health risk
- **Older adults (65+)**: Standard BMI thresholds may not apply
- **Children**: BMI must be compared to age- and sex-specific growth charts

For a more complete picture, consider also measuring waist circumference and body fat percentage.
