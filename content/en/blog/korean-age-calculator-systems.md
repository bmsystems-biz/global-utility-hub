## The Age Gap That Confuses Everyone

Meeting someone from Korea and being told they are "29" when your calculation says "27"? This is the Korean age system at work. Understanding it eliminates a surprising amount of social confusion.

## Side-by-Side Comparison

Take someone born on December 31, 2000:

| Age System | Calculation | Age on Jan 1, 2026 |
|------------|-------------|---------------------|
| 세는나이 (Korean) | Born = 1, +1 each New Year | 27 |
| 만나이 (International) | Born = 0, +1 on birthday | 25 (birthday not yet reached) |
| 연나이 (Year age) | 2026 − 2000 | 26 |

A person born on December 31st becomes 2 years older in Korean age the very next day — they go from 1 to 2 as midnight strikes.

## Where Each System Is Used

**만나이 (International)**
- Official documents, contracts, medical records
- Legal thresholds (age of consent, drinking, military service)
- Passport, national ID

**세는나이 (Korean traditional)**
- Everyday conversation
- School peer groups (everyone born in the same year is "friends")
- Traditional ceremonies

**연나이 (Year age)**
- Military service calculations (conscription timing)
- Some welfare benefit eligibility
- School enrollment cutoffs

## Why the Traditional System Developed

The Korean counting system has roots in East Asian lunar calendar traditions, where being born into a year meant you were "in your first year of life" — hence starting at 1. The new year symbolized a shared birthday for everyone.

## Practical Tips for Non-Koreans

When a Korean person tells you their age in conversation, they're likely using 세는나이. To get their international age, subtract 1 or 2 depending on whether their birthday has passed this year. The Korean Age Calculator does this automatically.
