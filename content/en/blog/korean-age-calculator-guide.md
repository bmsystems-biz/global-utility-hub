## Why Does Korea Calculate Age Differently?

If you've ever met a Korean person and noticed that their stated age seems to be 1 or 2 years older than what you'd calculate based on their birth year, you've encountered the Korean age system. Korea has traditionally used a system where people are born as "1 year old" and gain a year every New Year's Day — not on their birthday.

## The Three Korean Age Systems

### 1. 만나이 (International/Western Age)
- Same as the global standard
- You are 0 years old at birth
- You gain 1 year on your birthday
- **Example**: Born August 2000 → July 2026 = 25 years old (birthday not yet reached)

### 2. 세는나이 (Korean Traditional Age)
- You are 1 year old at birth
- You gain 1 year every January 1st
- **Example**: Born August 2000 → 2026 = 27 years old

### 3. 연나이 (Year Age / Administrative Age)
- Simply: current year − birth year
- Ignores the birthday entirely
- **Example**: Born August 2000 → 2026 = 26 years old

## The 2023 Korean Age Unification Act

On June 28, 2023, South Korea passed a law standardizing all official and legal age references to **만나이 (international age)**. Before this law, different government regulations used different age systems, causing confusion. Now:

- All official documents use 만나이
- Medical records and insurance use 만나이
- Legal age thresholds (drinking, voting, military) use 만나이

Informal conversation still often uses 세는나이, but official contexts are now standardized.
