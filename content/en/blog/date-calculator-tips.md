## Date Calculations Are Trickier Than They Look

Simple-sounding date questions — "how many days until my lease expires?" — hide several potential pitfalls. Here are five things to get right every time.

## Tip 1: Check Whether the Start Date Is Included

"7 days from today" — does today count?
- **Legal/contractual**: Usually the start date is excluded unless stated otherwise
- **D-Day countdowns**: Today is typically D-0 (included)
- **Delivery estimates**: Often counted in business days from the following day

When precision matters, always verify how the context defines the start.

## Tip 2: Account for Leap Years

When calculating across February 28th, check whether the year is a leap year (February has 29 days). A leap year occurs every 4 years, except for century years, unless divisible by 400.

## Tip 3: Watch Time Zones for International Dates

International contracts and transactions can span date lines. For example, an event "ending at midnight EST" could fall on a different calendar day in Seoul (UTC+9, which is 14 hours ahead of EST).

## Tip 4: Business Days vs. Calendar Days

Bank transfers, legal filing deadlines, and government services often count in business days, which exclude weekends and public holidays. The same 7-day deadline could be 9 or 11 calendar days depending on the week.

## Tip 5: Months Have Different Lengths

"2 months from March 31" has no direct equivalent (May has only 31 days, but there's no March 31 analog in May). Be explicit: use a date calculator to find the exact date rather than relying on "add 2 months."

## Real-World Applications

- **Lease renewal**: Calculate your exact move-out date from your lease start
- **Savings maturity**: Find the precise date your term deposit matures
- **Parental leave**: Determine available leave duration from birth date
